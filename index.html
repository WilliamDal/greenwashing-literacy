Greenwashing Literacy

01 / Definition

What is greenwashing?

Greenwashing is misleading or overstated environmental communication. It can appear in advertising, packaging, institutional statements, reports, public relations, social media posts and campaign slogans.

Not every sustainability claim is false. The literacy task is to ask whether a claim is specific, evidenced, scoped and honest about limits.

Vague product claim

"100% planet kind"

A warm phrase may sound responsible without saying which impact has improved, compared with what, or by how much.

Transition claim

"Powering today, building tomorrow"

A both-and statement may present expansion and transition as equally compatible without showing the pathway.

Energy-security claim

"Keeping the lights on for decades to come"

A security frame may turn long-term dependence into common sense while leaving alternatives unexamined.

02 / Change

Why greenwashing has changed

Contemporary greenwashing is not only vague green packaging. It can also work through climate-delay framings: accepting that climate change matters while making slow or inadequate action feel reasonable.

Lamb et al. (2020) identify four broad delay families. In public-facing terms, they help us notice how a message can appear realistic while narrowing what counts as possible.

1

Redirect responsibility

Move attention away from institutions, systems or producers and towards individual choices alone.

2

Push non-transformative solutions

Promote improvements that sound decisive but do not alter the underlying high-emission model.

3

Emphasise the downsides of action

Make climate action feel too costly, disruptive or unfair while giving less attention to the costs of delay.

4

Surrender

Suggest that meaningful change is impossible, too late, or unrealistic enough to excuse inaction.

03 / Tool

The four-question tool

The tool slows a claim down. It does not ask you to be suspicious of everything. It asks you to look at what the communication includes, what it leaves out, and what kind of future it makes feel normal.

01

What is claimed?

Identify the exact promise, benefit or impression. Separate measurable statements from mood words.

Look for
Scope, comparison, time frame, product versus company, absolute versus relative language.
Mini example
"Lower impact" only becomes readable when you know lower than what and across which impact.
Warning sign
The central words feel positive but cannot be measured or tested.
02

What evidence is offered?

Check whether the communication gives enough evidence for the scale of the claim.

Look for
Named data, independent verification, methods, dates, baselines and accessible sources.
Mini example
A recycling claim is stronger when it states actual collection access and material recovery limits.
Warning sign
The evidence is a logo, nature image or broad value statement rather than substantiation.
03

What is omitted?

Ask what relevant information would change how the claim is understood.

Look for
Lifecycle impacts, offsets, exclusions, trade-offs, supply chains, absolute emissions and rebound effects.
Mini example
A "plastic-free" pack may still rely on high-impact materials or hard-to-recycle coatings.
Warning sign
The claim highlights one improvement while avoiding the main source of impact.
04

What future is normalised?

Notice the future the claim invites you to accept as sensible, inevitable or desirable.

Look for
Promises of later action, permanent demand, technological rescue, consumer responsibility and "realistic" delay.
Mini example
"By 2050" may be meaningful, or it may move responsibility into a future without near-term milestones.
Warning sign
The message makes continued high impact feel unavoidable while describing action as optional.
04 / Practice

Try it yourself

Choose a fictional claim, then open the four lenses. The aim is not to guess a perfect answer. It is to make the claim more inspectable.

Card A / Product-level green claim

EverKind Wash Tabs

"100% planet kind cleaning, now in a lighter refill."

Small-pack convenience. Refill available online.

0 of 4 lenses opened

The phrase suggests the whole product is environmentally benign, while "lighter refill" points to packaging weight only.

No data is given for ingredients, transport, washing temperature, packaging recovery or comparison with the previous pack.

The claim omits which impact category improved and whether the refill system is actually available to most buyers.

Possible family: push non-transformative solutions, if a narrow packaging improvement stands in for wider product impact.

Card B / Both-and transition claim

Northbridge Energy

"Powering today's needs while investing in tomorrow's clean technologies."

Future portfolio language. No spending split shown.

0 of 4 lenses opened

The claim presents present supply and future clean investment as a balanced transition story.

The investment is unnamed. The claim does not show spending share, emissions trajectory, project dates or retirement plans.

It omits whether high-emission activity is expanding, stable or declining while clean technologies are described.

Possible family: push non-transformative solutions, especially if future investment delays near-term reduction.

Card C / Energy-security claim

HarbourGrid Supply

"Reliable domestic energy to keep the lights on for decades to come."

Reliability frame. Alternatives not named.

0 of 4 lenses opened

The claim connects long-term supply with safety, reliability and national reassurance.

It gives no evidence about demand, alternatives, resilience, price risk or compatibility with climate targets.

The phrase omits how energy security could also be served by efficiency, storage, grid upgrades and lower demand.

Possible family: emphasise the downsides of action, if change is framed mainly as a threat to reliability.

05 / Response

From critique to response

The aim is not cynicism. A stronger literacy habit ends with a constructive move: rewrite the claim more transparently, or ask one public scrutiny question.

Original

"100% carbon neutral by 2050"
Build a more transparent version

Better

Scrutiny question: What emissions, products, places and time periods are included in this claim, and what is left outside it?

06 / Educators

For educators

This resource can support a 60 to 90 minute activity for students, sustainability learners or programme teams. It works best as a guided reading exercise, not a test of personal virtue.

  1. Warm-up trust question

    Ask what makes a public sustainability claim feel trustworthy.

  2. Four-question tool

    Introduce claim, evidence, omission and future as a shared vocabulary.

  3. Real or regulator-validated case

    Use a careful public example where evidence has already been assessed.

  4. Fictional group cards

    Let groups practise without accusing named organisations.

  5. Individual reflection

    Invite students to name one question they would ask next time.

Facilitators should avoid personal disclosure pressure and keep the focus on public communication, not personal guilt. A short session can build useful habits of scrutiny, but it should not be overclaimed as transformation.

08 / Resources

Resources

Selected starting points for understanding greenwashing, climate delay, media literacy and sustainability education.

Greenwashing and environmental claims

  • Delmas and Burbano (2011), "The Drivers of Greenwashing". A clear account of organisational incentives behind misleading environmental communication.
  • Nemes et al. (2022), "An Integrated Framework to Assess Greenwashing". Useful for mapping greenwashing across claims, evidence and organisational context.
  • Competition and Markets Authority (2021), "Green Claims Code". Public guidance on truthful, clear and substantiated environmental claims in the UK.
  • Eng et al. (2021). Reading on greenwashing, consumer trust and sustainability communication. Useful for connecting claim design with audience interpretation.

Climate delay and fossil-fuel communication

  • Lamb et al. (2020), "Discourses of Climate Delay". The source for the four delay families used in this resource.
  • Supran and Oreskes (2017), "Assessing ExxonMobil's Climate Change Communications". A method-rich example of comparing corporate records with public-facing messaging.
  • Supran, Rahmstorf and Oreskes (2023), "Assessing ExxonMobil's Global Warming Projections". A peer-reviewed example of checking historical projections against later climate observations.
  • Bandura and Cherry (2020), "Enlisting the Power of Youth for Climate Change". Helpful for thinking about agency, efficacy and public engagement.

Media literacy and misinformation

  • Hobbs (2010), "Digital and Media Literacy: A Plan of Action". A practical foundation for asking who made a message, for what purpose and with what evidence.
  • NAMLE (2007), "Core Principles of Media Literacy Education". A concise statement of inquiry-led media literacy practice.
  • Lewandowsky and van der Linden (2021), "Countering Misinformation and Fake News Through Inoculation and Prebunking". Relevant to teaching people to recognise persuasive techniques before they recur.

Transformative learning and sustainability education

  • Mezirow (2000), "Learning as Transformation". A key text for critical reflection and changing frames of reference.
  • Bryan (2020). Climate change education work on emotion, difficult knowledge and the limits of simple awareness-raising.
  • UNESCO (2017), "Education for Sustainable Development Goals: Learning Objectives". A widely used framework for sustainability learning outcomes.
  • QAA and Advance HE (2021), "Education for Sustainable Development Guidance". UK higher education guidance for embedding sustainability in curricula.
09 / FAQ

FAQ

Is every sustainability claim greenwashing?

No. Many claims are useful, specific and well evidenced. Greenwashing literacy helps readers distinguish stronger claims from vague, selective or overstated ones.

Is this about blaming consumers?

No. The focus is public communication. Consumers and students should not be made responsible for solving every sustainability problem alone.

Can greenwashing be legal but still misleading?

Sometimes a claim may avoid a clear legal breach while still giving an unbalanced impression. This resource is educational and does not provide legal advice.

Why use fictional examples?

Fictional examples let learners practise the method without turning a workshop into a debate about a named organisation. Real cases can be added when they are carefully sourced or regulator-validated.

Can educators use the four-question tool?

Yes. Educators can adapt it for short classroom activities, media analysis, sustainability modules or staff development sessions.

How is this different from carbon literacy?

Carbon literacy focuses on understanding carbon impacts and actions. Greenwashing literacy focuses on reading sustainability claims, climate-delay framings and evidence in public communication.

Greenwashing Literacy is a public-facing educational resource for critical reading of sustainability claims.
